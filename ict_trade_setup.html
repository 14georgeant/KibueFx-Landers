Market Structure and Price Action Insights from ICT’s “Elements of a Trade Setup”

Abstract

This paper explores key insights on market structure and price action derived from the Inner Circle Trader (ICT)'s educational video titled “Elements of a Trade Setup.” It unpacks the core building blocks of institutional trading perspectives, analyzes how smart money leaves footprints on the chart, and discusses how traders can use these insights to frame high-probability setups in the forex and futures markets. This paper is specifically geared toward serious traders seeking precision and logic behind market movements.

1. Introduction

Michael J. Huddleston, also known as ICT (Inner Circle Trader), is known for his institutional-level insights into price delivery mechanisms. In “Elements of a Trade Setup,” he dissects the anatomy of a high-probability trade by focusing on market structure, liquidity, imbalances, and timing. This paper condenses those teachings, emphasizing actionable insights grounded in price action rather than indicators or lagging systems.

2. Market Structure: The Foundation of Context

2.1 Defining Market Structure

  • Bullish Structure: Higher highs (HH) and higher lows (HL)
  • Bearish Structure: Lower highs (LH) and lower lows (LL)
  • Consolidation: Range-bound (Equal Highs - EQH / Equal Lows - EQL)

2.2 Structural Shifts (Breaks in Market Structure - BOS)

  • Watch for BOS after a liquidity run (e.g., a raid)
  • Align BOS with daily or weekly bias for higher conviction

2.3 Premium and Discount Arrays

ICT’s Fibonacci-based model divides price legs into:

  • Premium: Above the 50% level (ideal for shorts)
  • Discount: Below the 50% level (ideal for longs)

3. Price Action Insights: Understanding the Language of Price

3.1 Liquidity Pools

  • Buy-side Liquidity (BSL): Above recent highs
  • Sell-side Liquidity (SSL): Below recent lows

Price often raids liquidity before making a directional move.

3.2 Fair Value Gaps (FVGs)

A three-candle imbalance zone where the middle candle creates an inefficiency. These act as retracement zones for entries.

3.3 Order Blocks (OBs)

The last up or down candle before a significant move. Entries are high probability when:

  • Inside a FVG
  • Aligned with premium/discount logic

4. Elements of a Trade Setup: ICT's Criteria

  1. Directional Bias – HTF market structure and daily bias
  2. Liquidity Raid – Sweep of external liquidity (EQH/EQL)
  3. Market Structure Shift (BOS) – Confirmation of intent
  4. Entry Model – Price returns to FVG or OB in favorable zone
  5. Timing – Aligned with killzones (London Open, NY Open)

One missing element reduces trade probability. The setup is a system of confluence.

5. Application in Futures and Forex Trading

  • Avoid trading the middle of the price leg
  • Anticipate false breakouts (liquidity raids)
  • Wait for BOS confirmation
  • Execute only during key sessions (e.g., NYSE Open)

Example: A WTI Futures trader may wait for a sweep of a weekly high during the NYSE open, followed by a 5-minute BOS and a retracement entry into a 1-minute FVG.

6. Common Mistakes and Misinterpretations

  • Chasing price after BOS without pullback
  • Misidentifying order blocks
  • Trading against daily bias without confirmation
  • Entering trades too early, before liquidity is cleared

7. Conclusion

ICT’s “Elements of a Trade Setup” offers a structured blueprint for understanding market logic. By focusing on structure, liquidity, and imbalance, traders can align with smart money behavior and avoid retail traps. The key is confluence and discipline—waiting for all conditions to line up.
